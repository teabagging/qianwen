🔑:The fundamental point of disagreement between Bayesian and frequentist approaches lies in their interpretation of probability and how this shapes their methods of statistical inference:1. Probability Interpretation: - Frequentists define probability as the long-run frequency of events. For example, a 95% confidence interval means that in repeated experiments, 95% of such intervals would contain the true parameter. Probability is strictly tied to observable, repeatable events. - Bayesians interpret probability as a subjective degree of belief or uncertainty about propositions. A 95% credible interval directly asserts a 95% probability that the parameter lies within the interval, conditioned on the observed data.2. Treatment of Parameters: - Frequentists treat parameters as fixed, unknown quantities. They do not assign probabilities to parameters, focusing instead on the likelihood of data given fixed parameters (e.g., maximum likelihood estimation). - Bayesians treat parameters as random variables with probability distributions (e.g., priors and posteriors). This allows them to quantify uncertainty about parameters directly.3. Use of Prior Information: - Bayesians explicitly incorporate prior knowledge or beliefs via prior distributions, which are updated with data to form posterior distributions. Critics argue this introduces subjectivity. - Frequentists avoid priors, relying solely on the data and its sampling distribution. They emphasize objectivity but cannot systematically integrate external information.4. Conditioning on Data: - Bayesians condition on the observed data to update beliefs, leading to intuitive probabilistic statements about parameters. - Frequentists evaluate procedures (e.g., confidence intervals, p-values) over hypothetical repetitions of experiments, focusing on error rates rather than direct parameter probabilities.In summary, the core disagreement stems from whether probability represents objective, repeatable frequencies (frequentist) or subjective degrees of belief (Bayesian). This distinction cascades into their methodologies, influencing how parameters are treated, how evidence is assessed (e.g., p-values vs. Bayes factors), and the role of prior information in analysis.
